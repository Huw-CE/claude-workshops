Prompting · 10 min

Stop chatting.
Start briefing.

Claude isn't Google. Vague prompts get vague answers. The more context you give — the project, the audience, the constraint, the format — the better the output. Think of it like briefing a very smart, very fast, completely literal senior designer who has never worked at Charles Elena before.

Plan Mode

Think before you build.

Before any complex task, type "think step by step" or use Claude's plan mode. Claude will map out its approach before doing anything. This is especially useful for multi-step tasks like writing a campaign brief, structuring a presentation, or auditing a design system. You can review, redirect, and approve before Claude touches a single output.

How plan mode works

Claude proposes → you review → Claude executes.

When you activate plan mode, Claude doesn't do anything until you approve. It shows you what it intends to do — step by step — before touching a single output. If the plan is wrong, redirect it. If it's right, approve and watch it go. This is especially powerful for tasks that touch multiple files, multiple ideas, or multiple people's work.

Live Activity · 10 min

Same brief. Different results.

Everyone open Claude and type the same prompt: "Write a creative brief for an AI-powered photo booth experience at a tech conference." Don't add anything else. Then we'll compare outputs across the room — and see what happens when you add client context, a target audience, and a constraint. Spoiler: it's a different document.

The Rules

Three things that change everything.

1

Always set context first.

Tell Claude who you are, what the project is, and who the audience is before asking anything. Claude has no idea it's working at Charles Elena unless you tell it.

2

Give constraints, not just topics.

"Write a tagline" is weak. "Write three tagline options for a Gen Z audience, under eight words, that avoid tech jargon" is strong. Constraints are not limitations — they're instructions.

3

Iterate, don't rephrase.

If the output is wrong, tell Claude why — don't just ask again differently. "That's too formal" beats "try again." "The tone needs to feel more like a DM than a press release" beats both.
